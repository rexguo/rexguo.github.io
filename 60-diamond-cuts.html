American cut

Same as ideal cut round brilliant according to the proportions introduced by Henry D. Morse in the USA in the early twentieth century as a result of his experiments since the 1860s. The proportions were generally accepted in the 1930s as table size: 53.0%, pavilion angle: 40.75º, crown angle: 34.5º, crown height: 16.2%, pavilion depth: 43.1% and total height: 59.0%. The expression was later promoted by the Gemological Institute of America and American Gem Society since the 1930s. It is also the trade term associated for many years with the round brilliant proportions of Marcel Tolkowsky published in 1919.

brilliant cut

Cutting style especially developed to obtain the better compromise between brilliance, fire and scintillation in diamonds. It has 57 (or 58 facets if a culet is faceted), with 33 facets on the crown, 24 or 25 facets on the pavilion and the most common shape is round, with variations in oval, pear, navette (or marquise), heart and cushion. In diamond, the girdle may be faceted. It was originally developed in the seventeenth century with notable popularity since the mid-eighteenth century, havin g then been recognized as a distinct cutting style, known in the trade today as old mine cut. The late nineteenth century brought proportion and symmetry evolution, notably the mechanical bruting technology in the 1870s to produce round outlines, giving rise to what is known in the trade as the old European cut, whereupon further developments in the USA from 1860 resulted in the so-called American cut of the early twentieth century. Emerging from this in Europe from 1919 was what became known as the Ideal cut, according to Marcel Tolkowsky’s proportions. Since then, numerous modifications in proportions, symmetry and number of facets have been made, some as patented and registered cuts. The round brilliant cut, sometimes referred to as the modern brilliant cut, is not exclusive to diamond or diamond imitations and is the most popular cutting style and shape for diamonds in the present time.

Examples include the Orlov and the original Koh-i-Noor diamond cut in the Mughal period. rose cut

Collective name for a series of faceted, typically flat-bottomed cutting styles reportedly developed in the fifteenth centu ry but with wide usage, especially in the seventeenth century. It was mostly used for diamonds but also for coloured gemstones, particularly in the baroque period. It is typically a dome covered by triangular (more rarely kite-shaped) facets in various outlines in six-fold symmetry (sometimes four-fold or eight - fold), terminated by a point, often irregularly shaped in old cut stones (e.g. round, pear, oval, triangle). The number of facets and their arrangement defines the various types of rose cuts, althoug h there are overlapping descriptions reported in the literature: three near-triangular facets, six facets, 12 facets (Antwerp rose, flat rose), 18 facets (half- Dutch rose), 24 facets (e.g. Dutch rose, Antwerp rose, cross rose, full rose), 36 (double Dutch rose). Larger stones may exhibit a higher number of facets, often referred to as expanded Dutch rose cuts. It was the most important diamond cutting style until the mid- eighteenth century and became popular for coloured gemstones after the late seventeenth century. The briolette , as a double rose cut, is a drop-shaped variation of a rose cut. Although it is mostly seen in diamonds, it has also been used in coloured gemstones where a greater diversity in the complexity of the facet arrangement is well docume nted.
